About Me

Welcome! I am Nata, an astrophysicist specializing in high-resolution galactic-scale hydrodynamical simulations. My main interests are the formation of stars and star clusters in low-metallicity gas-rich environments that mimic the star formation conditions of the early Universe. Currently I'm based at the Max Planck Institute for Astrophysics in Garching near Münich, Germany, where I collaborate with Prof. Dr. Guinevere Kauffmann and Dr. Thorsten Naab. I received my PhD in 2020 at the University of Helsinki in Finland under the supervision of Peter H. Johansson. See my CV for more details and my ADS page for a complete list of publications.

Research

The interstellar medium (ISM) consists of three main phases that are the hot, low density medium, the warm (neutral or ionised) medium and the cold dense medium. The regions of cold gas occupy a tiny fraction of the volume of the ISM, yet they play a crucial role as the formation sites of all new stars. More often than not, stars do not form within these cold clouds alone but in clusters and associations. In the most extreme star formation environments, such as starburst, star clusters may form with masses of more than a million solar masses.

Modelling the interplay of gravity, hydrodynamical forces and feedback between stars and the multiphase gas in such extreme conditions in entire galaxies requires simulations that can only be executed on supercomputers. In my work I mainly use the SPHGal -code that is a parallelized tree/N-body code with smoothed particle hydrodynamics.

The GRIFFIN project

Our GRIFFIN project (Galaxy Realizations Including Feedback From INdividual massive stars) investigates the evolution of dwarf galaxies using state-of-the-art hydrodynamical methods. Our models resolve the multiphase gas down to low-temperatures using a non-equilibrium chemical network while modelling the feedback (photoionization, photoelectric heating, supernovae, winds) of individual stars. The collaboration gathers international expertise on the physics of the ISM, star formation, black holes and numerical techniques from Germany, Finland and the US. We use computational facilities such as the Freya cluster hosted by the Max-Planck-Computing and Data Facility and the SuperMUC-NG hosted by the Leibnitz Supercomputing Centre.

Check the project website (wwwmpa.mpa-garching.mpg.de/~naab/griffin-project) for more information about the methods, people, simulations and beautiful radiative transfer images and captivating movies of the science produced in the project!

Formation of massive star clusters

In my research I am interested in deciphering the formation of realistic globular clusters resolved with individual stars in full galactic-scale hydrodynamical simulations. Using SPHGal, we investigate the evolution of a variety of dwarf galaxy models where the gas-rich, low-metallicity ISM conditions emulate the star formation environments prevalent at and beyond the cosmic noon. Such environments are the birth sites of massive star clusters and the most massive star clusters forming in our dwarf starburst models evolve as would be expected for globular clusters (Lahén et al. 2019, 2020a,b).

In observed globular clusters, the origin of their chemical composition and the so-called multiple populations of stars remains an open question. Currently I am investigating the effect of winds and chemical enrichment by very massive stars in the cluster formation regions as a possible solution to the abundance anomalies in globular clusters.

Post-processing and mock observations

We have studied the population of star clusters forming in simulated dwarf starbursts and isolated dwarfs both using the full 3D information available in the snapshots, as well as through radiative transfer post-processing. In Lahén et al. (2022) we investigated how the hierarchical nature of the star cluster formation process complicates the separation of individual young clusters in images of cluster formation regions that are 2D projections of the true 3D structure.

A rotation around the radiative transfer processed dwarf starburst in B (blue), V (green) and I (red) bands at a HST resolution assuming the system is at a distance of 10 Mpc. The dense gas structures that follow the outline of the few hundred parsec wide supernova cavity obscure the most intense star formation region (as imaged above) in some of the projections.
